My main areas of research are political philosophy, PPE, and animal ethics. In political philosophy and PPE, I am especially interested in comparative institutional analysis (broadly construed) and its implications for normative democratic theory. In my dissertation, I draw upon findings from political science and other fields to investigate democracy’s ability to solve the problem of fundamental security: the problem of addressing fundamental threats to citizens’ lives and freedoms such as existential risk and political instability. Although contemporary democracies generally outperform contemporary non-democracies on several fronts, democratic decision-making is still significantly compromised by ignorance, irrationality, and short-termism, and it is unclear whether democracy would address fundamental social threats (and promote our various other political ends) better than all possible alternatives under all possible circumstances. I therefore argue that we should reject the idea (advanced by Rawls and others) that democracy is required by justice and instead adopt political experimentalism, an approach to political justice which permits a degree of experimentation with different political systems.


I am also currently working on a second interdisciplinary research program in animal ethics which explores the relationship between the ethics of eating meat and health. Most animal ethicists have given relatively little consideration to the possibility that meat (especially red meat) can be healthy. But a growing body of evidence suggests that meat can often promote significant improvements in human health. Suppose that is the case—what, if anything, follows ethically? The answer, I suggest, is that eating meat is permissible at least for those whose health would improve significantly by doing so—but not necessarily under all circumstances.


In upcoming work, I intend to develop both of these research programs further. I plan to build upon my dissertation by providing a more robust account and defense of political experimentalism. I also hope to explore the implications of different theories of social and political power for political philosophy. In addition, I intend to expand my research program in animal ethics by exploring the relationship between the ethics of eating meat and animal agriculture’s environmental impact.


Apart from these two main research programs, I also have research interests in explanations of normative change (especially liberalization); social contract theory, contractualism, and egalitarianism (especially in Rawls); and cryptocurrency, monetary policy, and justice.
