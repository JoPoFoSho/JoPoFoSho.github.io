My main areas of research are political philosophy and PPE. I am especially interested in comparative institutional analysis (broadly construed) and its implications for normative democratic theory. In my dissertation, I investigate democracy’s ability to deal with what I call the problem of fundamental security: the problem of addressing fundamental threats to citizens’ lives and freedoms like existential risk and political instability. Drawing upon a variety of findings from political science and other fields, I argue that democracy may not always be best suited to address such fundamental threats and that under some circumstances other political systems may possibly address them better. I therefore suggest that we should reject the idea that democracy is required by justice.


I am also currently working on a second interdisciplinary research program in animal ethics which explores the relationship between the ethics of eating meat and health. Most animal ethicists assume that eating meat is unnecessary for or even harmful to good health. However, a growing body of evidence suggests that meat can sometimes promote significant improvements in people’s health. I therefore suggest that eating meat is permissible at least for those who would experience such improvements in health from doing so.


In upcoming work, I intend to develop both of these major research programs further. I plan to build upon my dissertation by defending political experimentalism, an approach to political justice which permits (and even encourages) experimentation with different political systems to determine which ones best promote our various ends. I also hope to explore the implications of different theories of social and political power for political philosophy. In addition, I intend to expand my research program in animal ethics by exploring the relationship between the ethics of eating meat and animal agriculture’s environmental impact.


Apart from these two major research programs, I also have research interests in explanations of normative change (especially liberalization); social contract theory, contractualism, and egalitarianism (especially in Rawls); and cryptocurrency, monetary policy, and justice.
